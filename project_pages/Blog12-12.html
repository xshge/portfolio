Rapid Game Prototypes for 13 weeks, Part 1

09-28-2024

≡

This blog is part 1 of me going through all the protoypes that I created for my Core Game Design Class. Each week we would have a different theme for our protoypes, and there are some of my thoughts on the theme and the end result.

Link to all the games that is mentioned in this blog

Wk1 Weird Pong:

Honestly, I did not start off strong with the first week. Our theme was Weird Pong, basically Pong but reimagined with our own twist. I experimented with Unity’s 3D physics for a bit and tried to combine 2D elements with 3D ones. But, I have bitten more than I can chew, so the prototype of this week was completely incomplete. However, it did teach me a good lesson in how variable it is to have good time management.

Wk2 Wario-Ware Like:

This week was definitely better than last week, at least I had a complete prototype for the week. Due to the nature of a game like Wario-Ware, I did find that when functionality of a game is broken down into smaller chunks, it is easier to program said functions. From that week’s feedback, I also got the first taste of the importance of teaching players the skills for the game.

This struggle of effectively communicating the theme and the skill in game to the player will come up repeatedly throughout the rest of the weeks as well. On the bright side, I did come in with a plan so I was not super stressed about making this week’s game.

Wk3 Fidget:

This week was our first open theme week, and it definitely was a different experience than the two that came before this one. Since we had the free reign to interpret the theme, I thought of doing an anti-fidget game with inspiration from my favorite scene in movie, Caveat; The game was heavily audio and concentration based, so when it comes to the play test session, the condition was not the most ideal. I think the playtesting condition definitely hindered some part of the gameplay that is supposed to teach the player through the experience.

Also for this week’s attempt at teaching players the necessary skills, I tried the heavy instruction approach that some indie games have for their tutorials, and its effect was not super good. One noticeable occurrence is that players will forget the instructions, and that was probably because it was a lot to take in and there is no more assistance after starting the game. I knew that a game typically will add short instruction ui for intractable elements in game to combat this phenomenon, but I thought the game play was simple enough that I can get away from that and create some challenge for the players. Afterall, my main goal for this pedagogical process in my game is to follow a reinforcement learning pattern, as I personally dislike blatant tutorials in games.

Wk4 Photo Worthy:

This week is about making a game around aesthetics with a camera element in it. This theme was more difficult for me to come up with ideas for, as I typically take priority in the technical aspects of my game. I ensure that the game play is close to complete and mostly bug free, and aesthetics were left on the back burner mostly. I decided to go with the focus of the theme and directed the majority of my focus to the aesthetics of the game, so the gameplay in this game was pretty simple.

This week is not completely fruitless, as I learned new topics regarding Unity Camera Rendering, and I tried to make my first NPC “AI.” Moreover, I also got to experiment with shaders in Unity, as they have been a topic that I’m heavily interested in.
