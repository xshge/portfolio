Rapid Game Prototypes for 13 weeks, Part 2

10-20-2024

≡

This blog is part 2 of me going through all the protoypes that I created for my Core Game Design Class. Each week we would have a different theme for our protoypes, and there are some of my thoughts on the theme and the end result.

Last Part Link to all the games that is mentioned in thid blog

Wk5 Light Camera Games:

This week was not completely open themed but it still left us with plenty of room for exercising our creativity. We were asked to make a game based off of a video clip, and said video clip could be taken from anywhere. I chose a scene from The Boys where the protagonist used the lasers from a super-baby to get out of a rough situation. I thought that action could work into an interesting game play mechanic, and from feedback, people did enjoy it despite the absurdity of it. For this prototype I tried to introduce the mechanics and the controls through dialogues that the player can read at the beginning of the game. I think that approach is also not super effective as it has the similar issue with week three’s instructions, as the controls can get forgotten by the player, however this doesn’t hinder too much of the experience as the control is simple enough that player can pick it up quicker.

Wk6 In Season:

This week is also an open theme week, and for this one, I decided to make a narrative puzzle game based on the change of the season. This project was also the first game that had a puzzle in it, and frankly, the scope of this game, which ended up being too big for one week, hindered the development of the learning aspect of the game. In order for this game to embody its ideal final form, I would have needed more time as a large amount of time was spent on other mechanics and the environmental building for this game.

Due to the lack of playtesting on my own, there were major bugs found in the game when it came to the actual playtest session, and it was frustrating to see your own game just not work. Moreover, there were a lot of feedbacks on the player task being unclear, and there was a valuable lesson that came out of that failure, which is to reverse the process backward, from the most complex to the most simple version of your puzzle in development, as such the tasks become easier to manage.

Wk7 Pet Life:

During week seven, we were tasked to create a game around an autonomous digital life form that has its needs and its own actions. I wanted the game to be about a creature that is both a potential threat and a subject to be taken care of by the player. I think this dichotomy would be interesting for a pet life form. For this game, I decided to go with the route of a narrative tutorial that holds the player’s hand in teaching all the tasks and the mechanics of the game. Based on the feedback, it does work alongside the consistent instruction reminder UI. However, this is the type of tutorial that I dislike as it takes away part of the fun of figuring things out, and it breaks the immersion of the game a little. I do have to admit it does get the job done, and I see why a lot of games use it.
